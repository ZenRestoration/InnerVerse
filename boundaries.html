Pillar 04 — Boundaries

Boundaries: love with edges.

Boundaries are not walls to keep people out — they’re the edges that let you stay in your own life. This lesson gives you emotional and somatic boundaries, red flags, and scripts for saying “no” without hardening or abandoning yourself.

Emotional vs somatic boundaries.

Emotional boundaries protect your feelings, values, and time. Somatic boundaries protect your body and nervous system.

Emotional boundaries
  • Choosing what topics you’re available to discuss
  • Not taking responsibility for someone else’s mood
  • Refusing to be spoken to with contempt or cruelty
Somatic boundaries
  • How close someone stands or touches you
  • How much noise/stimulation you can handle
  • How late you’re willing to stay or how long you visit
Boundaries are information, not punishment.
Your “no” protects your “yes.”
You’re allowed to adjust your edges over time.

Red flags: when boundaries are needed now.

Some behaviours tell you it’s time to tighten, not explain harder.

  • They mock, belittle, or roll their eyes when you express your feelings.
  • You notice you always leave interactions feeling drained, anxious, or smaller.
  • They only respect your boundary when it benefits them.
  • You find yourself rehearsing conversations in your head for hours before seeing them.
  • They escalate, guilt-trip, or retaliate when you set even a small limit.

Red flags don’t require you to prove anything. They invite you to believe your body.

Boundary scripts (click a tab to view).

Use these as starting points and adjust to your tone, culture, and context.

Soft no: “Thank you for the invite. I don’t have capacity for that right now, but I appreciate you thinking of me.”
Soft limit with care: “I love you and I want to support you. Right now I can listen for about 10 more minutes, then I need to take a break for myself.”
Firm no: “I’m not available for topics like that anymore. If the conversation goes there, I’m going to leave the room.”
Firm limit with family: “I’ve shared this before, but I’ll be clear again: I’m not willing to be spoken to in that way. If it continues, I’ll end the call/visit.”
Broken-record boundary: “I understand you see it differently. I’m still not available for that.” (Repeat the same sentence calmly; no extra explaining.)
When they push back: “I hear that this is frustrating. My boundary is the same.” (You don’t have to debate your limits.)
Exit plan: “I’m going to head out now. This doesn’t feel good for me. We can talk again when it’s calmer.”
Exit without over-explaining: “This conversation isn’t working for me. I’m going to take some space and we can revisit it another time.”

Somatic signals that a boundary is needed.

Notice what your body does before your mind convinces you to stay.

  • Jaw clamps down, shoulders creep toward ears
  • Stomach dips, nausea or sudden fatigue
  • Feeling smaller, quieter, or strangely far away
  • Ignoring thirst, bathroom breaks, or hunger to keep the peace

These are not inconveniences — they’re data. Your nervous system is whispering, “Something here is too much, too fast, or not okay for me.”

Boundary practice: one step up, not ten.

Instead of going from “no boundaries” to “cut everyone off”, choose a 1-step upgrade:

  • From 3-hour visits → 1.5-hour visits.
  • From replying instantly → replying when regulated.
  • From explaining everything → one clear sentence.
  • From saying “it’s fine” → “it’s actually not okay with me.”

Boundaries are a practice, not a personality type. Every small edge you honor teaches your system: “I’m on my own side now.”
