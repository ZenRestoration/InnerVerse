Pillar 03 — Needs

Needs: the difference between soothing and truly being fed.

You don’t have “too many needs” — you have a nervous system that got used to going without. This lesson helps you understand what needs actually are, how they differ from coping, and how to communicate them clearly without shrinking, explaining yourself to exhaustion, or abandoning what matters.

What is a need, really?

A need is something your system requires to function and feel safe enough over time. A coping mechanism is something you do to survive the absence of that need.

Examples of needs
  • To feel emotionally safe and not mocked for your feelings
  • To have predictable alone time to decompress
  • To be spoken to with respect, even in conflict
  • To know what’s expected of you (clarity)
  • To feel wanted, not just useful
Examples of coping
  • Shutting down instead of saying you’re hurt
  • Working nonstop to avoid feeling replaceable
  • People-pleasing so no one gets upset
  • Checking their phone every 5 minutes for reassurance
  • Using substances or distractions to not feel lonely

Needs create long-term nourishment. Coping creates short-term relief.

Needs are not demands.
Coping is a clue, not a flaw.
You’re allowed to be well-fed, not just “fine.”

Emotional, relational, and self-needs.

Toggle between them and notice which list hits your body the most.

  • To have your emotions welcomed, not shut down or mocked
  • To feel like you’re “allowed to exist” even when you’re not productive
  • To have space to feel without being rushed to “get over it”
  • To experience joy and play, not just survival
  • To trust that someone will show up when they say they will
  • To be able to express concerns without being punished
  • To know where you stand in a relationship (no constant guessing)
  • To receive care, not just give it
  • To speak to yourself with the same tone you use for people you love
  • To honour your body’s limits instead of overriding them
  • To allow rest, pleasure, and creativity without earning them first
  • To hold your own hand when others can’t meet you yet

Needs vs expectations.

Expectations are often unspoken rules we hold others to. Needs are truths about what our system requires to stay well.

Expectation

“You should text me back immediately or you don’t care.”

Underlying need

“I need to feel that I matter to you and that I’m not being ignored.”

Moving from “should” language to “need” language reduces shame and defensiveness on both sides and gets you closer to the real conversation: what lets this relationship feel safe enough for both of us?

How to communicate needs without apologizing for them.

Use this simple structure: context → feeling → need → request.

Script template: “When <SITUATION> happens, I feel <EMOTION>. I realize I need <NEED>. Would you be willing to <SPECIFIC REQUEST>?”
Example (predictability): “When plans change last minute, I feel anxious and unimportant. I realize I need more predictability. Would you be willing to give me as much notice as you can, even if it’s just a quick text?”
Example (emotional safety): “When I share something vulnerable and the topic changes quickly, I feel brushed off and alone. I realize I need a bit more space for my feelings. Would you be open to pausing for a moment and reflecting back what you heard before we move on?”

Identifying needs underneath coping.

Choose one coping behaviour and walk it through this mini-journal:

  • 1. Coping: What do I do when I’m overwhelmed? (scroll, work, drink, fix others, etc.)
  • 2. Relief: What feeling does it give me for a moment? (numb, in control, not alone)
  • 3. Need: What might I actually need? (rest, reassurance, being held, clarity, structure)
  • 4. Micro-request: What is one small way I could ask for or give that need to myself?

Remember: asking for a need is an invitation, not a guarantee. Other people are allowed to have limits. You are still allowed to have needs — and to make choices based on whether those needs can be met.
