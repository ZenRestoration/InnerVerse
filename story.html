Lesson Two

The Narrator Within

Every human has an inner storyteller — part historian, part bodyguard. It wants to keep you safe, but sometimes it forgets you’ve grown.

Thoughts as ancient protectors

Your mind’s first job isn’t happiness — it’s survival. It tries to predict pain and prevent it. That’s why old beliefs cling on: they once protected you. Awareness lets you thank them and update them.

Wallpaper analogy

Beliefs become background — like wallpaper you stop noticing. Awareness is stepping back and asking, “Did a younger me pick this pattern?” If yes, you can choose a new one without shaming the old you.

Micro-practice: Spot the sentence

  • Recall a recent reaction.
  • Write the first sentence your mind told you (exact words).
  • Ask, “Is this current or a memory?”

Separating the observer from the narrator is freedom’s first step.

Update the prediction, don’t fight the mind

Arguing with thoughts locks you in. Updating predictions frees you. Offer your mind new data gently: present safety cues, small wins, supportive people, clear plans.

Script examples

  • Old: “If I’m visible, I’ll be attacked.” Update: “Some spaces weren’t safe. This one is chosen and bounded.”
  • Old: “I’m always too much.” Update: “I’m learning dose and audience. My depth is a gift.”
  • Old: “I’ll fail again.” Update: “I’ve failed and learned before. I can start small and iterate.”

Practice: Gentle cross-examiner

Ask: “What is this story trying to protect? What else could be true now?”
