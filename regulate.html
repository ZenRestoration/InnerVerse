Module 02 — Regulate

Regulate your state: coming back to “safe enough.”

Goal: give you a menu of realistic, non-performative tools you can actually use in the middle of real life to come back to “safe enough.” Not perfect, not zen — just enough safety to have choice again.

Overwhelm is a body event first.
Regulation is not performance.
Safe enough is a win.
Lesson 1 Understanding your signals

Overwhelm happens in the body first.

Core idea: overwhelm is a body event first, a thought problem second. Your body usually knows long before your mind catches up.

1. Overwhelm happens in the body first

Typical early body signals:

  • you stop breathing fully or start holding your breath
  • your shoulders lock up or creep toward your ears
  • your jaw clenches, teeth press together
  • your stomach flips, clenches, or drops
  • your hands go numb, cold, or tingly

If you only listen to your thoughts, you miss the early warning system — and end up noticing only once you’re already in a spiral.

2. Early cues to watch for

  • Tight chest → “Something feels threatening.”
  • Rapid thoughts → mind trying to control every possible scenario.
  • Numbness / floaty feeling → system overloaded, shutting some circuits off.
  • Jitteriness → extra adrenaline, urge to move, escape, or talk nonstop.
  • Tunnel vision → hyper-focus on one problem, can’t see context.
  • Urgent need to run or fix → cancel everything or send That Big Message.

Micro-examples:

  • You open your banking app → your chest tightens → you immediately want to close it and avoid.
  • You see “Can we talk?” on your phone → your stomach drops → your mind sprints into worst-case scenarios.

3. Why your system activates

  • Past experiences: similar tone, expression, or situation = “I know this; it was dangerous before.”
  • Perceived threat: not just physical (car crash) but emotional (rejection, conflict, criticism).
  • Emotional memory: your body remembers more than your conscious mind.
  • Sensory overload: noise, lights, demands, messages, clutter — all piled together.

Knowing this helps you shift from “I overreact to everything” to “My system is doing exactly what it was trained to do.”

Reflection prompts

  • “Which physical cues show up first when I’m overwhelmed?”
  • “What situations send my system into chaos the quickest?”
  • “How do I currently know I need a break — and how often do I ignore that signal?”
Exercise — Body Signal Log Once or twice a day, complete: “I noticed my body doing ____. My mind said ____. I chose to ____.” Example: “I noticed my heart racing and my stomach dropping when I saw my dad’s name on my phone. My mind said, ‘You’re in trouble; this will be bad.’ I chose to let it ring, take 10 breaths, and call back later when I felt steadier.” Over time, this log becomes your personal nervous system map.
Lesson 2 Coming back into your body

Regulation is helping your body believe you’re safe enough to choose.

Core idea: regulation is not “calming down to be good.” It’s helping your body believe you’re safe enough to have options again.

1. Why regulation works

Your nervous system is constantly asking:

  • “Am I safe?”
  • “Can I rest?”
  • “Do I need to defend?”

Regulation techniques are ways of answering: “Right now, in this room, I’m safe enough to slow down.”

2. Core practices (with where / when to use them)

a) Slowed exhale breathing

Good for: anxiety, anger, racing thoughts.

  • Inhale for 4, exhale for 6–8.
  • Do 5–10 cycles.
  • Great in the car, bathroom, bed, or on a walk.
b) Grounding through senses

Good for: spiralling thoughts, panic, dissociation.

  • 5 things you can see.
  • 4 things you can feel.
  • 3 things you can hear.
  • 2 things you can smell.
  • 1 thing you can taste.
c) Movement + shaking

Good for: anger, adrenaline, restlessness, freeze.

  • Shake limbs for 30–60 seconds.
  • Walk around the block, stretch, or dance to one song.
  • Think of animals shaking after a scare — you’re helping your body complete that cycle.
d) Orientation (look around the room)

Good for: feeling trapped, flashbacky, or unsafe without clear reason.

  • Slowly scan the space.
  • Name objects and colours.
  • Remind yourself: “I’m in my kitchen / room / car. It’s <time>. I’m here now.”
e) Touch (self-soothing)

Good for: shame, sadness, loneliness.

  • Hand on chest + slow exhale.
  • Hand on back of neck.
  • Hugging a pillow or stuffed animal.
  • Weighted blanket or wrapped in a blanket cocoon.
Choosing in context

You don’t have to do everything. One small practice is enough to begin shifting your state.

3. Choosing the right tool for the moment

  • If you feel wired & anxious → slowed exhale + senses.
  • If you feel empty & numb → gentle movement + orientation.
  • If you’re furious → shaking + movement first, then breath.
  • If you’re ashamed → touch + gentle words: “I’m with you. This is hard, and you’re not a monster.”

Reflection prompts

  • “What actually helps me when I’m anxious — not what I think should help?”
  • “What has brought me out of shutdown in the past?”
  • “Which tools feel grounding and which feel like ‘too much work’?”
Exercise — The 3-Step Reset Whenever you feel on edge or reactive: 1) Pause output • Don’t send the text. • Don’t answer yet. • Don’t keep arguing. 2) Return to the body • Choose one tool: breath, senses, movement, orientation, or touch. • Do it for at least 60–90 seconds, even if your mind complains. 3) Offer simple words “I’m here. I don’t have to fix everything in this minute. One small step is enough.” Aim for a 10–20% shift, not total zen.
Lesson 3 Your personal regulation plan

Generic advice isn’t enough. You need your own menu.

Core idea: everyone’s system is different. You need a personalized list of what reliably soothes you — in your body, mind, and heart.

1. Create a list of what soothes you

Start by dividing into three areas:

Body

Hot shower or bath, stretching, short walk, dancing to one song, curling up in blankets, lying on the floor, swimming, gentle yoga, feeling sunlight on your skin.

Mind

Journaling, brain dump, reading, puzzles, gentle show, calming music, ambient noise, silence, guided meditation, writing lists of “done” instead of “to-do”.

Heart

Voice note to a friend, hugging a pet, talking to someone safe, prayer or ritual, drawing or painting, singing, meaningful quotes, revisiting memories that remind you you’re loved.

2. Plan for your patterns

If you already know your go-to responses, build around them:

  • If you overwork when anxious → plan a hard stop time, put it in your calendar, and choose a body soothing activity for that moment.
  • If you people-please when scared → have 1–2 boundary sentences pre-written, like: “I really appreciate you asking, but I don’t have capacity for that right now.”
  • If you shut down when criticized → plan a phrase such as: “I’m overwhelmed. I need a break and I want to come back to this.”

3. How to regulate in relationships

Regulation gets stronger when you let others in on your patterns.

  • “When I’m overwhelmed, I might go quiet. I’m not punishing you; I’m regulating.”
  • “If I say, ‘Can we pause?’, it means I’m trying not to shut down completely.”
  • “Sometimes I might need a break from my phone. It helps if you know I’ll reply when I’m able.”

This builds co-regulation instead of confusion and guessing.

4. Repairing your system after activation

After a blow-up, panic, or spiral, you can still tend to your system:

  • Body first → shower, walk, breath, stretch, eat something grounding.
  • Meaning next → journal: What happened? What did I feel? What did I need?
  • Communication last → when regulated enough, share with the other person if needed.

Reflection prompts

  • “What brings me back to myself reliably?”
  • “What soothing things have I been withholding as ‘rewards’ instead of basic care?”
  • “What do I wish others understood about what overwhelm looks like in me?”
Exercise — Build your “State Shift Kit” Create a simple list: • 5 things that calm your body • 5 things that calm your mind • 5 things that reconnect you to the present • 3 people you can reasonably reach out to for support Put this list: • in your notes app, • on a sticky note by your bed, • or as a screenshot background. This becomes your go-to menu when your mind goes blank in the middle of a storm.

Bringing it together: your regulation in real life.

Regulation is not about never getting activated again. It’s about noticing earlier, responding with care instead of shame, and having a few simple, practiced tools ready for when life inevitably gets loud.

Each time you choose one small regulation step, you’re teaching your system: “I’m with you. We’re not alone in this anymore.”
