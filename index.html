The four foundations of emotional education.

From 0–100 years old, the core skills are the same: name what you feel, understand why it’s there, regulate your body, and take responsibility for your impact. The details live on the other Mynd pages — here’s the simple overview.

The four emotional skills Mynd teaches

  • 1. Emotional Literacy · “Name what you feel.”
    Most people only know “good / bad / fine.” Literacy is learning to say: “My chest is tight, my thoughts are racing — I think I’m anxious,” or “My body feels heavy and slow — I think I’m sad or burnt out.”
  • 2. Emotional Intelligence · “Understand what it’s saying.”
    Emotions are dashboard lights, not defects. Anxiety might say “I don’t feel safe enough yet.” Anger might say “A boundary was crossed.” Sadness might say “I lost something and need comfort.”
  • 3. Emotional Mastery · “Respond instead of react.”
    You learn to pause and ask: “What does this state need by 5–10%?” — breath, space, movement, a smaller to-do list, or asking for help, instead of doom-scrolling, lashing out, or shutting down.
  • 4. Emotional Maturity · “Own your side of the street.”
    Your feelings are real — and they’re your responsibility. Maturity sounds like: “When that happened, I felt hurt and I shut down. That part is on me. I’m sorry. Here’s what I’m changing.”

What this looks like in real life:

Example · Teen after school

They slam their door and say “Leave me alone.” Most adults call it “rude.” Inside, their stomach is tight and their mind is replaying something embarrassing.

  • Literacy: “I’m not just ‘moody’ — I feel anxious and ashamed.”
  • Intelligence: “My anxiety is saying ‘That thing at school felt unsafe.’”
  • Mastery: “I need 10 minutes alone and then I can talk about it.”
  • Maturity: “I’m sorry I snapped. I was overwhelmed. Can I tell you what happened?”

Example · Adult in a relationship

Their partner is late again. The first reaction is “I’m so angry.” A quick body scan shows a tight chest, lumpy throat, and the thought “I don’t matter.”

  • Literacy: “I’m not just angry — I feel hurt and unimportant.”
  • Intelligence: “My anger is telling me a value or boundary was crossed.”
  • Mastery: Take a breath, unclench the jaw, choose not to text a rage paragraph.
  • Maturity: “When you’re late and I don’t hear from you, I feel worried and then unimportant. Can we agree on a quick ‘home safe’ text?”

This is the same loop Mynd teaches across all pillars — State, Patterns, Needs, Boundaries, and Integration.
