Module 04 — Integrate

Relate with integrity: bringing your inner work into real life.

Goal: take everything you’ve learned inside and translate it into how you speak, set boundaries, repair, and choose relationships. This is where your inner clarity becomes outer self-respect.

Speak from your experience, not from attack.
Boundaries as self-respect, not punishment.
Conflict as repairable, not a death sentence.
Lesson 1 Speaking from yourself

Honest communication without attacking or disappearing.

Core idea: you can speak honestly about your inner world without attacking, begging, or disappearing. You stay with yourself while you speak.

1. Communicating without blame

Blame & self-erasure
  • Blame: “You never listen.”
  • Self-erasure: “It’s fine, it’s just me being sensitive.”
Self-responsible

“When I share and don’t feel responded to, I feel invisible and hurt.”

Here you own your experience without making statements about their entire character.

2. Sharing your inner world clearly

Use a simple structure:

  • Situation — what actually happened.
  • Feeling — emotion words, not accusations.
  • Meaning / need — what it touches in you or what you’re needing.

Example:

“When our plans change last minute, I feel anxious and unimportant. I need more predictability where we can.”

3. Vulnerability without collapse

You can:

  • share your hurt
  • request change
  • and hold onto self-respect — all at once.

Instead of:

“Please don’t leave me; I’ll change everything.”

Try:

“It really hurts when this happens. I care about us and need ____ to keep feeling safe here.”

4. Clarity instead of reactivity

Before you speak, ask:

  • “Am I trying to punish, prove, or connect?”

If you’re trying to punish or prove, regulate first. If you’re trying to connect, you’re closer to integrity.

Reflection prompts

  • “How do I usually communicate when I’m hurt?”
  • “Which part of me comes out: attacker, explainer, or avoider?”
  • “What would it look like to speak honestly while also respecting myself?”
Exercise — The Honest Sentence Use: “When ____, I feel ____. What I hope for is ____.” Examples: “When you cancel last minute, I feel anxious and unimportant. What I hope for is more notice when it’s possible.” “When I’m interrupted repeatedly, I feel dismissed. What I hope for is us taking turns to speak.” Let this become your default conflict template — short, honest, and self-respecting.
Lesson 2 Boundaries as self-respect

Edges that protect your energy and allow real connection.

Core idea: boundaries are how you take care of yourself while still leaving the door open for connection where it’s safe.

1. Boundaries = personal edges

They answer:

  • “What am I okay with?”
  • “What am I not okay with?”
  • “Under what conditions can I stay present here?”

2. Boundaries protect energy, not punish people

Examples:

  • “I won’t discuss my body/weight with anyone.”
  • “I don’t stay in conversations where I’m being yelled at.”
  • “I won’t respond to texts after 10pm unless it’s an emergency.”

These are not attacks; they’re policies for your well-being.

3. Expressing boundaries clearly & kindly

Short, clear, calm:

  • “I’m not available for that topic.”
  • “I can’t lend money. I’m happy to help you brainstorm options.”
  • “I’m not able to come this weekend. I hope it goes well.”

4. Maintaining boundaries under pressure

Expect some people to:

  • guilt-trip (“You’ve changed.”)
  • minimise (“You’re overreacting.”)
  • test (“Just this once?”)

Your job is to repeat calmly:

“I understand you feel that way. My boundary is still the same.”

Reflection prompts

  • “Where in my life do I feel consistently drained or resentful?”
  • “What am I saying ‘yes’ to that I wish I could say ‘no’ to?”
  • “What would one simple, honest boundary look like in that area?”
Exercise — Your Boundary Script Fill in: “I’m not able to ____. Here’s what I can offer instead: ____.” Examples: “I’m not able to talk about your relationship for hours anymore. Here’s what I can offer: 15 minutes, and then I need to switch topics or hop off.” “I’m not able to come over when you’re drinking. I’m happy to see you during the day or when things are calmer.” This helps you honour your limits without abandoning the relationship altogether.
Lesson 3 Rupture & repair

Conflict as a place to grow, not proof it’s over.

Core idea: conflict doesn’t mean everything is broken. The absence of repair is what breaks trust.

1. Why repair matters

Without repair
  • lingering tension
  • walking on eggshells
  • distance and quiet resentment
With repair
  • “We can go through hard things and still be okay.”
  • nervous system learning that conflict ≠ abandonment
  • deeper trust over time

2. Owning your impact

You can:

  • validate their experience
  • acknowledge what happened
  • share what you’ll try next time

…without saying, “I’m the worst person alive.”

3. Rebuilding trust

Trust grows through:

  • small consistent actions
  • aligning words and behaviour
  • repeating repair over time

It’s okay if it’s slow.

4. Repair without collapsing or blaming

Avoid:

  • “Fine, it’s all my fault then.” (collapse)
  • “If you weren’t so sensitive, we wouldn’t even have a problem.” (deflecting)

Aim for:

“I see that what I did hurt you. I care about that and want to do better.”

Reflection prompts

  • “What do I usually do when I realize I’ve hurt someone?”
  • “What fears come up around apologizing (being wrong, being rejected, losing power)?”
  • “Where in my life do I wish someone had repaired with me?”
Exercise — Repair Script Use: “I can see how what I did impacted you. I care about you and that wasn’t my intention. Next time, I’m going to try ____. Thank you for telling me.” Example: “I can see how raising my voice scared you. I care about you and that wasn’t my intention. Next time, I’m going to take a break before it gets to that point. Thank you for being honest with me.” Practice this even in small moments so your nervous system learns that repair is possible and survivable.
Lesson 4 Choosing connected, healthy relationships

Letting your healing shape who gets close.

Core idea: your healing includes choosing who gets close to you.

1. Knowing who gets access to you

Not everyone gets:

  • your inner world
  • your time
  • your emotional labour

You can love people from varying distances.

2. Healthy vs draining dynamics

Healthy-ish signs
  • You can say “no” without being punished.
  • You both apologize sometimes.
  • You feel seen and respected more often than not.
  • You can bring up issues and eventually work through them.
Draining / unhealthy signs
  • You regularly feel smaller, crazier, or guilty.
  • Your boundaries are mocked, ignored, or punished.
  • They only care about your feelings if it benefits them.
  • You leave interactions feeling anxious, shaky, or ashamed most of the time.

3. Identifying relationships that can grow

Things to look for:

  • They can hear feedback without exploding or stonewalling every time.
  • They show genuine remorse and change when they hurt you.
  • They respect your “no” even if they’re disappointed.
  • You both feel safe enough to be imperfect.

4. When to stay, step back, or leave

  • Stay & invest when there’s basic safety and mutual effort.
  • Step back when your body constantly tenses around them or you feel chronically drained.
  • Leave when there is ongoing harm, disrespect, or your nervous system never feels safe.

Leaving doesn’t mean you hate them. It means you’re choosing life over constant survival mode.

Reflection prompts

  • “Who in my life feels genuinely nourishing and safe?”
  • “Who consistently leaves me confused, small, or on edge?”
  • “What kind of relationships am I committed to building in this next chapter?”
Exercise — Relationship Audit Make two lists: List A: “People who help me grow / feel safe / feel seen.” List B: “People I feel small, stressed, or unsafe around.” Then ask: • Who deserves more of my presence? • Who deserves less? • What is one concrete boundary or change I can experiment with in the next month? This is not about judging people as “good” or “bad” — it’s about locating where your nervous system can actually heal.

Bringing it all together.

Integration is not perfection. It’s letting your awareness of state, regulation, patterns, and needs quietly shape how you speak, what you allow, how you repair, and who you walk with.

Every honest sentence, every small boundary, every attempt at repair, and every choice to move closer to nourishing people is you living in integrity with your nervous system — not against it.
