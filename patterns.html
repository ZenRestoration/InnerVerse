Pillar 02 — Patterns

Patterns: the loops that run your life until you see them.

Emotional patterns are not evidence that you’re broken — they’re evidence that your system adapted. Here we map childhood conditioning, roles, and self-sabotage cycles so you can choose something new.

What is an emotional pattern?

An emotional pattern is a repeated sequence of sensation → thought → behaviour that your system learned keeps you safe (or less unsafe).

  • Trigger: something reminds your body of past danger.
  • State: you shift into fight/flight/freeze/fawn.
  • Story: your mind explains what’s happening based on old data.
  • Action: you react in familiar ways, even if they hurt now.

Patterns feel like “this is just how I am” — until you name them and see their origin.

Childhood conditioning & emotional roles.

Many of your adult patterns started as childhood jobs: roles you took on to keep connection, stability, or invisibility.

The Responsible One

Grew up managing chaos or emotions at home. Pattern now: over-functioning, burnout, feeling guilty when you rest.

The Peacemaker

Mediated conflict, kept everyone okay. Pattern now: avoiding hard conversations, agreeing outwardly while resenting inwardly.

The Invisible One

Learned it was safer to be quiet/unnoticed. Pattern now: shrinking, not asking for help, feeling overlooked.

You might recognize several roles. They’re not life sentences — just starting points for compassion.

Common loops (tap to expand).

Self-sabotage & “almost there” cycles
›

You work hard, get close to what you want, then “randomly” shut down, pick a fight, overspend, or disappear.

Underneath is often a belief like, “If I actually succeed, I’ll be more visible / more alone / more pressured.” Sabotage is protection in disguise.

Trauma loops & emotional flashbacks
›

Your body reacts to current events with the intensity of the past. You know logically you’re safe, but your system is convinced you’re not.

These are not overreactions — they’re proportional to what your system remembers. Naming them as “flashbacks” can be relieving.

Over-thinking & catastrophizing
›

The brain tries to think its way out of feelings. Planning every possible scenario was once a survival skill.

When you notice spirals, try: “My mind is creating options. I don’t have to believe all of them.”

Avoidant vs anxious cycles in relationships
›

One person pursues, the other distances. The more one chases, the more the other retreats — and vice versa.

These are not “good vs bad” styles — they’re nervous system strategies. Naming the dance is the first step to changing it.

Triggers: the doorway into your pattern.

Triggers are not proof you’re weak. They’re proof your system is still holding unprocessed material.

  • Notice what always sets you off: tone, silence, mess, late replies, etc.
  • Ask, “Where have I felt this before?” (earlier in life, another relationship)
  • Ask, “What did I have to do back then to stay safe or connected?”

That “old job” is probably still running.

Pattern-mapping exercise (journal-friendly).

Use this template with one specific situation in mind:

  • 1. Trigger: What actually happened? (Keep it factual.)
  • 2. State: Which state did I go into? (fight/flight/freeze/fawn)
  • 3. Story: What did I tell myself it meant about me/them?
  • 4. Action: What did I do next?
  • 5. Origin: Where does this feel familiar from in my past?
  • 6. Need: What did I actually need right then?

Even doing this once a week starts creating distance between you and the loop.
